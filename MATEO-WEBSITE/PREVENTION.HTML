Prevention



Preventing a heart attack isn’t about big, dramatic changes — it’s about everyday habits that protect your heart over time. It begins with making smart choices in your lifestyle and staying consistent with your health goals.

Start with your diet: Choose foods that are low in saturated fat, trans fat, salt (sodium), and added sugar. Instead, fill your plate with fresh vegetables, fruits, whole grains, nuts, fish, and lean meats. Think of food as fuel for your heart — what you eat directly affects your arteries, cholesterol, and blood pressure.

Physical activity is the next powerful tool. Moving your body for at least 30 minutes a day, five times a week, can help strengthen your heart muscle, improve circulation, lower your cholesterol, and control your weight. Whether it’s walking, dancing, biking, or swimming — what matters most is consistency.

Avoid smoking completely, and if you already smoke, seek support to quit. Smoking damages your blood vessels, reduces oxygen in your blood, and increases your risk of blood clots — a major trigger for heart attacks. Another key factor is managing stress. Chronic stress can raise blood pressure and trigger unhealthy habits like overeating or smoking. Practice relaxation techniques like deep breathing, meditation, journaling, or even short breaks from screens and noise.

Make sure to get enough sleep, ideally 7–8 hours per night. Poor sleep can affect your hormones, raise blood pressure, and increase the risk of obesity and diabetes — all of which increase heart attack risk. If you have existing conditions like high blood pressure, high cholesterol, or diabetes, it’s critical to manage them properly. Take your medications as prescribed, attend regular check-ups, and follow your doctor's advice.


Lastly, know your numbers. Stay informed about your blood pressure, cholesterol levels, blood sugar, and body mass index (BMI). When you’re aware of your risk factors, you can take action early — and early action saves lives.
